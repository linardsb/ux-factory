## Product

Verdant is a plant-care companion for people who own houseplants and keep losing them. It holds
a record of every plant — species, spot in the home, watering rhythm — and turns that into a
short daily check-in: what needs water today, what's due for feed, what looks off. One glance,
two minutes, plants stay alive.

## Users

Urban plant owners with five to twenty plants and no system — care lives in memory and guilt.
The named persona is **Rita, 34**, a product manager with eleven plants across three rooms. She
loves them, forgets them in busy weeks, and has thrown away enough dead ferns to feel bad about
it. She doesn't want a smart-home hub or moisture sensors; she wants to stop guessing.

## Problem

Plants die from *irregular* care, not lack of love. Watering is easy; remembering which plant
was watered when, across a dozen different rhythms, is not. Today Rita solves it with memory,
an occasional phone reminder labeled "plants??", and sticky notes that fall off the shelf. Each
works for a week and decays. The replacement need is a single place where the rhythms live, so
the person only has to show up.

## Behaviour model

The one behavior Verdant wants to make routine is the **daily check-in**: open the overview,
see what needs attention, act on it or consciously defer. Realistic frequency is near-daily —
care actions themselves are weekly per plant, but with ten-plus plants on staggered rhythms,
*something* needs attention most days. That puts the behavior inside the habit zone (Hooked's
frequency filter, Ex 1.5–1.6): frequent enough that a designed trigger–action–reward loop can
take hold, and the design is justified in building one.

The internal trigger is the low-grade worry Rita already feels walking past her plants —
"am I forgetting one?" The check-in resolves that worry in one glance; the variable reward is
of the *self* kind (Hooked's mastery/nurture register): visible streaks of healthy plants,
a struggling plant recovering, a collection thriving because she showed up.

## Ethics position

The frequency filter passes and the Manipulation Matrix (Hooked Ex 6) places Verdant in the
**facilitator** quadrant: it materially improves users' lives — plants genuinely stay alive —
and the maker would use it daily. Verdict: **habit-justified**. Building a habit loop here is
designing *for* the user's own goal, not against it. This is the scenario where the method says
yes — its counterpart, Fieldwork, is the scenario where the same method says no.
